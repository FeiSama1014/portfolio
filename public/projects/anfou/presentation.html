Area of Opportunity

How might we reduce the friction of staying in touch, so checking in feels like a habit rather than an obligation?

We have more communication tools than ever, yet reaching out feels harder. Between close family, even sending a simple "I'm okay" can feel like it needs more. The closer the relationship, the greater the pressure to respond properly. So we say nothing.

Not from a lack of care, but from the weight of it.

Personal Connection

This project comes from my own daily experience. As an international student in Toronto, far from my family, I leave my mom's messages unread for hours. Not because I stopped caring, but because even "I'm fine" feels like it needs more energy than I have at the end of the day.

She never asks me to call more. She just wants to know I woke up safe. And I want the same for her.

The Paradox

We are surrounded by tools for staying connected. Messaging apps, video calls, social feeds. Yet more and more people find that reaching out has become something they put off. This is especially common between close family members. The closer the relationship, the more pressure we feel to respond properly, to catch up, to say something meaningful.

Existing Solutions

  • Messaging apps (WhatsApp, WeChat, LINE) turn check-ins into social tasks requiring a reply
  • Safety apps (Life360, bSafe) feel clinical and surveillance-oriented, not caring
  • "Dead Yet?" app proved massive demand but its shock-value branding alienates older users
  • All depend on stable internet connectivity

The Gap

Between "social media" and "emergency alert" there is room for something quieter, warmer, and more accessible.

Something built around emotional comfort, not crisis response. Accessible regardless of age, language, or internet connectivity.

Research + Bias

Assumptions & Bias Management

Initial Assumptions

People living alone often feel anxious when they are temporarily unreachable

A lack of simple communication increases worry for both the individual and their trusted contacts

Existing safety or check-in solutions feel too complex or intrusive for everyday use

Elderly and international students have very different comfort levels with technology

Close relationships carry more communication pressure, not less

Biases Identified & Mitigation

Personal bias

Assuming safety anxiety is experienced the same way across different age groups and cultural backgrounds

Technology bias

Assuming all users are comfortable with smartphone-based solutions and notifications

Solution bias

Leaning toward simplicity before fully understanding whether users prefer automation or manual control

Cultural bias

Assuming communication norms are similar across Chinese, Japanese, Korean, and Canadian contexts

Mitigation Strategy

Listen to lived experiences rather than validating pre-existing ideas. Avoid leading questions. Validate insights across two distinct focus groups.

Qualitative Persona

Yuki Tanaka

Age: 23  ·  She/Her
Toronto, ON, Canada
Graduate Student, Computer Science
Single  ·  Japanese

The Quiet Worrier

CHANNELS & TOOLS

  • LINE for family (mostly her mom)
  • Instagram for browsing, rarely posts
  • Discord for a small classmate group
  • Prefers async communication over calls
  • iPhone user, relies on Google Calendar

BIO

Yuki moved to Toronto from Osaka for her master's degree. She has always been on the quieter side, someone who prefers small groups, texts over calls, and needs time alone to recharge. Moving abroad amplified this. She finds daily social interaction in a second language draining, and by the end of the day she has no energy left to reply to anyone, including her mom. Her mother sends a LINE message almost every morning. Yuki sees them hours later and knows she should reply, but the longer she waits the more it feels like she needs to say something more. So she puts it off again.

GOALS

  • Let mom know she is safe without composing a reply
  • Reduce the guilt of leaving messages unread
  • Know her mom is okay without initiating a call
  • Low-energy connection that respects her need for space

FRUSTRATIONS

  • Even simple replies require social energy she runs out of
  • The longer she delays, the harder it gets to respond
  • Video calls feel overwhelming, she avoids them
  • Worries about mom but asking directly feels awkward

MOTIVATIONS

  • Deeply cares about her mom despite the silence
  • Values independence but not at the cost of connection
  • Drawn to tools that are simple and non-intrusive
  • Motivated by reducing guilt and anxiety

EMPATHY MAP

SAY

"I will reply later" (then does not)
"I am fine, just busy"
"I don't want her to worry but I can't keep up"

DO

Opens LINE, reads message, closes without replying. Sets reminders to call home, often skips them. Sends short replies late at night out of guilt.

SEE

Unread LINE messages piling up from mom. Classmates who seem to call home easily. Apps that demand more interaction, not less.

😣 PAINS

Guilt cycle: delay leads to pressure leads to more delay. Feels like a bad daughter. Social energy is finite, family gets the leftovers.

😊 GAINS

Would feel relieved if mom knew she was okay without a text. A no-effort check-in would break the guilt cycle. Alarm-based would catch her at the right moment.

I don't want to ignore her. I just wish 'I'm okay' didn't have to be a conversation.

Yuki's mother. They are each other's primary contact in AnFou.

BIO

Megumi works three days a week at a local library in Osaka. The rest of her time is quiet. Since Yuki left for Canada, the apartment feels emptier than it used to. She sends Yuki a message every morning on LINE, just something small so Yuki knows she is thinking of her. Most days Yuki replies hours later, sometimes not until the next day. Megumi understands, she remembers being young and busy, but the waiting still makes her anxious. She would never say this to Yuki. She does not want her daughter to feel guilty or obligated.

GOALS

  • Know Yuki is safe every day without asking directly
  • Let Yuki know she is okay without it feeling like a demand
  • Have something notice if she does not get up one morning
  • Maintain her independence and dignity

FRUSTRATIONS

  • Anxious when Yuki does not reply, but hides it
  • Most apps are too complex for what she needs
  • Does not want to seem needy or become a burden
  • Home internet unreliable during typhoon season

MOTIVATIONS

  • Wants to protect Yuki from worrying about her
  • Values simple tools that do one thing well
  • Cares deeply about her daughter despite distance
  • Quietly fears something happening with no one knowing

EMPATHY MAP

SAY

"She is just busy, I understand"
"I do not want to bother her"
"As long as she is healthy, that is enough"

DO

Sends a morning message to Yuki every day. Checks phone multiple times waiting for reply. Hides her anxiety so Yuki does not feel guilty.

SEE

An empty apartment, especially in the evenings. LINE messages marked as read but no reply for hours. News stories about accidents or loneliness.

😟 PAINS

Waiting hours with no reply creates quiet anxiety. Afraid of being a burden to her own daughter. Worries what would happen if she fell and no one knew.

😊 GAINS

A simple signal from Yuki each day would ease her worry. Something that works even when internet is down. Feeling connected without the pressure of conversation.

I never ask her to call more. I just want to know she woke up safe today.

Research + Bias

Research Triangulation

Three independent research methods converged on the same core findings, each compensating for the blind spots of the others.

MARKET RESEARCH

Comparative Analysis

Analyzed 4 products across 8 dimensions: daily check-in, offline support, emotional tone, elderly accessibility, privacy, mood tracking, and nudge systems.

Finding:

A clear gap exists. No product combines emotional warmth, minimal friction, and offline capability.

QUALITATIVE RESEARCH

Personas & Empathy Maps

Two personas developed as a mother-daughter pair. Empathy maps revealed shared pain from opposite directions: Yuki's pressure to reply vs. Megumi's anxiety of waiting.

Finding:

Both want the same thing but existing tools force a conversation neither has energy for.

BEHAVIORAL TESTING

User Flow Validation

Assumptive flows tested against real behavior. 4 out of 5 testers forgot manual check-in by day two. Alarm integration moved from secondary to core feature.

Finding:

Caring is not the problem. Remembering is.

Core Insight

The problem is not about technology. It is about the emotional cost of communication. The solution must cost zero effort, or it becomes part of the problem.

Ideation, Testing + Refinement

Design Principles

Established theory guided every interaction decision in AnFou.

Hick's Law

One button, one action. No menus, no choices to weigh. The check-in screen presents a single button. Fewer decisions means faster, more reliable daily use.

Fogg Behavior Model

Motivation + Ability + Prompt at the same moment. The alarm is the prompt. Dismissing it is the ability. Caring is the motivation. All three converge at 7:30 AM.

Progressive Disclosure

Megumi sees only the alarm. Yuki can explore mood stats and history. Complexity is available but never forced. Each user gets the depth they want.

Inclusive Design

Built for the least capable user first. Large font toggle, SMS fallback, minimal interaction. Accessibility is the default, not an afterthought.

Ideation, Testing + Refinement

Testing & Iteration

Assumptive designs were tested with representative users. Feedback was integrated into validated versions.

ISSUES IDENTIFIED

Manual check-in fails quickly. 4 out of 5 participants forgot to open the app by day two. Caring is not the issue, remembering is.

Alarm setup buried in settings. Users who discovered the alarm path found it easier, but most never found it during onboarding.

Emergency alert too abrupt. "My mom would panic if she got that message without warning." The 48h direct alert felt too sudden.

Mood felt mandatory. Two testers skipped check-in entirely because they did not want to pick an emoji. No action available when contacts missed days.

CHANGES IMPLEMENTED

Alarm-first onboarding. Alarm setup moved into onboarding flow, framed as "the easiest way to check in."

Primary path redesigned. Alarm check-in is now the recommended path. Manual remains as secondary. Zero apps opened.

Soft nudge with call button. Before emergency alert, contacts see "3 days without check-in" with a one-tap "Call?" button.

Mood optional + accessibility. Mood made skippable. Added large font toggle and dark mode for accessibility.

Final Solution

Why AnFou Works

⏰

Alarm = Check-in

Dismiss your morning alarm. That's it. Zero apps opened, zero effort, zero friction. The habit is built into something you already do.

💬

SMS-First

Server-routed when online, native SMS when offline. Works for grandma in rural Japan during typhoon season.

🔔

Gentle Nudge

"3 days without check-in. Call her?" Guides action instead of triggering panic. Care, not crisis.

♥

Inclusive by Default

Large font, dark mode, multi-language. Built for the least capable user first. Accessibility is not an add-on.

13 / 15
Reflective Practice

Reflection

How the design process shaped the project, what worked, and what comes next.

Strongest Part

The mother-daughter persona pair forced every decision to serve two opposite needs simultaneously. Yuki would never open an app daily. Megumi would never learn a complex one. The alarm integration solved both: it is invisible for Yuki and effortless for Megumi.

Weakest Part

User testing relied too heavily on peers and classmates. More diverse participants, especially elderly users and people in rural areas with poor internet, would have provided stronger validation.

Biggest Obstacle

Balancing simplicity with completeness. Every feature was weighed against one filter: does this make check-in easier, or does it add another reason to put it off?

Next Steps

Real user testing with elderly participants across countries. Exploring family groups beyond pairs. Investigating wearable integration for even more invisible check-ins.
